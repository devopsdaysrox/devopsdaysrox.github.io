To understand anti-patterns and how they are created, we must first understand the limits of peoples ability to deal with information. The human mind can only do so much:

Leading grassroots organizational change

Leading change is hard. Very often there's a perspective that you need to have a title to lead change. Chip Digirolamo (Sonatype) - @chipdigi

The Next Big Borrow - Software Supply Chain Automation

In almost every market, software has become critical. Software is how we display our innovation and establish a competitive advantage. We see this at Uber, who have disrupted the taxi industry without owning any cars. They do it all with software. Dollar Shave Club have used their software to create a better customer experience and disrupt the razor industry. To deliver software at the pace of innovation, apps are no longer written, they are assembled with prewritten components. The software development process has evolved into one that closely resembles the assembly lines of traditional hard good. This reliance on 3rd party components has made the quality of each component a priority. Most companies lack visibility into the quality of the component they use and regularly introduce security and license risk into their applications without knowing. Many ignore component quality until they are faced with a "must fix" emergency. The problem becomes one of inventory management and is addressed by following 3 supply chain principals.

  • Use fewer and better suppliers
  • Use only the best of their products
  • Track what you use and where

By embracing these principals you bring hygiene and discipline to your supply chain which will:

  • Reduce costly rework caused by low quality components
  • Reduce "break/fix" emergencies that "stop the line"
  • Reduce the time to diagnose and fix the problem when such emergencies can't be avoided

How's your software supply chain?

Crisis Communication Breakdown

Our process for communicating during an outage is most definitely a work in process, which is common for many startups at our stage. But when you’re talking about handling the alerts of our customers, we need to be especially sensitive of how and when we inform our stakeholders about an outage. In this talk, I’ll share a few of the things we’ve learned in our journey to be better at connecting with our customers during a firefight.

  • Crisis Communication is scary! but your customers will reward honesty, candor and timeliness with loyalty
  • Buy-in: you need it at the top and at the bottom
    • People need to agree on:
    • What gets communicated
    • Who is managing the message
    • Where the message is getting shared (key accounts, Twitter, StatusPage...)
    • When, and how often, alerts and updates go out
  • We have a cross-functional Crisis Communication team that gets pulled into incidents as they occur
  • Members of the Crisis Communication team attend “official” crisis communication training & get certified
  • We manage Crisis Communication during a firefight without disrupting the critical workflow o Crisis Communication can help troubleshooting in a firefight, by ensuring that everyone involved has the same understanding of symptoms and causes
  • This is DevOps:
    • We retrospect, iterate, and make continuous process improvements
    • We do cross-functional post-mortems, so developers have an awareness of Crisis Communication and vice-versa
    • We use ChatOps and our own toolset to get the Crisis Communication team involved during incidents

Claudia Ring (IBM DevOps Marketing) - @claudia_ring

A DevOps Necessary Evil: Marketing

Before you start your pilot, think about it a bit with your newly assembled DevOps team; what are the biggest pains you face in your roles, what do you think you can actively work together to change during the pilot

  • Put down on paper what those bottlenecks are, how long things currently take and how many people are involved
  • Measure and track progress during your pilot - have a 5 minute stand up daily to talk about what is going well, what is changing and what hasn't, and any quantifiable changes (i.e. it only took an hour to get a VM today to test vs 3 weeks, or whatever)
  • When your pilot is done and you have a real deliverable to show for it, whether it's a fix pack or a new app, consolidate those metrics and seek out the best 'nuggets' to promote - wherever you can cite the biggest changes
  • If you need to, get someone from sales or marketing to help you put it together in a way that 'tells a story' - yes, I realize that is a marketing term, but it works when you tell the before and after story in a cohesive manner, dropping in numbers where they make the most impact
  • If you manage to sell this to your management and they want you to think about broader implications, reach out to some other groups, ask them what problems they are facing, and line those up with any ROI calculations you may have to create those larger/org wide estimates for time or money savings
  • Don't forget about the emotional aspects of selling this; if you can fathom a guess that the release team might be able to avoid x weekends in the office, you can include the implications for their home life, job satisfaction and any other qualitative factors along with the time and risk reduction (management usually wants to keep reliable, hardworking people there)
